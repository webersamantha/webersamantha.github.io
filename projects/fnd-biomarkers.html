Featured Project 03

Biopsychosocial Biomarkers in FND

This project grew out of my PhD thesis and focused on one core question: how can multimodal imaging and psychobiological data help us understand functional neurological disorder as a real, measurable, and still profoundly understudied condition?

Status: PhD Thesis Project Domain: Neuropsychiatry Focus: Multimodal Imaging in FND Methods: rs-fMRI, CAPs, Structural Imaging
Intro Multimodal Imaging Co-Activation Patterns Selected Papers

1. Why This Work Mattered

Functional neurological disorder has been neglected for far too long. For many patients, the burden of the illness is amplified by the fact that they are not taken seriously, are passed between specialties, or are treated as if the absence of a simple lesion means the absence of a biological basis. After working with these patients directly, it became important to me to contribute rigorous evidence that FND deserves the same scientific attention as other brain-based disorders.

The goal of this thesis was not to reduce FND to a single marker, but to study it as a biopsychosocial disorder with measurable signatures across brain dynamics, structure, stress-related biology, and clinical presentation. That framing shaped the whole project: use multimodal methods to ask where stable traits, transient states, and clinically meaningful subgroups can be detected.

2. Multimodal Imaging in an Understudied Disorder

A major part of the thesis focused on resting-state fMRI because FND is fundamentally about altered brain function, network coordination, and symptom expression in the absence of classic structural lesions. The work spans BOLD signal variability, multicentre classification from resting-state connectivity, biopsychosocial trait markers, and salivary oxytocin with amygdalar alterations. Together, these studies examine whether FND can be characterized through converging evidence rather than a single isolated imaging result.

  • Resting-state fMRI: used to capture altered network organization without imposing a task structure on patients.
  • Dynamic metrics: BOLD signal variability and time-varying network approaches were used to move beyond static connectivity.
  • Structural imaging: integrated in the biopsychosocial markers work to connect brain organization with broader trait-level vulnerability.
  • Psychobiological context: oxytocin-related and amygdalar findings helped link neural alterations to stress, salience, and socio-affective processing.

3. Why the Co-Activation Pattern Papers Are Central

Three of the core papers use co-activation pattern analysis. That matters because static averages can miss the transient brain states that may be especially relevant in FND. Co-activation patterns let us study how large-scale systems briefly assemble and reassemble over time, which is a better fit for a disorder marked by fluctuating symptoms, altered salience processing, and state-like changes in control and self-agency.

The transient resting-state salience-limbic paper, the locus coeruleus co-activation work, and the altered brain network dynamics paper collectively point toward FND as a disorder of abnormal network persistence, switching, and coordination rather than a single damaged region. This dynamic perspective is one of the most important conceptual contributions of the project.
